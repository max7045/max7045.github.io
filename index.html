Kneeling during the national anthem

By Max Zeiler & Jan Schuler

Published September 21, 2023

Kneeling during the national anthem

It is tradition in sports that when the national anthem of a country is played, its athletes stand up to show allegiance to their country and their flag. But what happens, when they don’t want to show allegiance because racism and police violence are so prevalent in their country that they can no longer stand behind its values?

In the first two games of the NFLs Preseason in 2016 the San Francisco 49ers Quarterback, Colin Kaepernick, was seen not standing up, while the national anthem was played. Even though he wasn’t playing these two games, reporters started asking questions about his behavior and when confronted with the question why he didn’t want to stand up during the national anthem, this was his reasoning:

“I am not going to stand up to show pride in a flag for a country that oppresses black people and people of color, to me, this is bigger than football and it would be selfish on my part to look the other way. There are bodies in the street and people getting paid leave and getting away with murder.”
– Colin Kaepernick

The next week, Kaepernick decided to kneel down while “The Star-Spangled Banner” was played. In doing so he was joined by his fellow team member Eric Reid. This started some kind of movement, where many NFL players started to kneel down to protest racial discrimination and oppression. This new form of protest called “taking the knee” got a lot of negative backlash and even former president Donald Trump commented, saying he would love to see NFL owners fire any athlete who kneels down during the national anthem.

“Wouldn’t you love to see one of these NFL owners, when somebody disrespects our flag, to say, ‘Get that son of a bitch off the field right now. Out! He’s fired. He’s fired!’””
– Donald Trump

Trump's harsh opinion on the topic had a lot of supporters who argued that showing this kind of disrespect to the US and their flag, dishonoring what their ancestors had built, shouldn't happen without consequences. Furthermore the fact that there are soldiers oversea, fighting for the US and their values, was a major argument for critics. They stated that disrespecting the flag and the USA while others are dying for this country and are trying to protect it, was a disgrace for the NFL and the whole country.

Barack Obama expressed his concern about the topic by saying that, even though the message was very important and people should start to look at problems like racial discrimination, police violence and institutionalized racism closer and try to change things, the way the athletes were delivering the message could distract people from Kaepernick's original intentions. Thus instead of creating awareness for the real problems, the footballers were creating a new one by splitting the country in two groups, one saying that “taking the knee” is a legitimate way of peaceful protest and is an important way to show the underlying problems of racism in the US and the other one opposing these statements and criticizing that dishonoring the flag and the country is the wrong way to create awareness for these topics.

In May 2018 NFL Clubs decided to fine players who were “taking the knee” during the national anthem, but they allowed the players to stay in the locker rooms while “The Star-Spangled Banner” was played. However some players like Eric Reid said that they will remain to kneel down, even if they get fined for doing so.


But where does “taking the knee” originate from?

Kneeling has a long history in the black rights movement. Martin Luther King, one of the most famous civil rights activists of all time, knelt down in 1965 during a march in Selma (Alabama) while leading a prayer in front of the Dallas County Courthouse. The march was held to protest unfair voting rights for black people and followed the killing of Jimmy Lee Jackson, a black civil rights activist, who was shot by a police officer whilst participating in a peaceful protest in Marion, Alabama.
But kneeling actually goes back way further. A drawing of an enslaved black man kneeling down, with a scroll that reads “Am I not a man and a brother”, from the 1780s became the emblem of the British abolitionist movement in the 1800s.
Rinaldo Walcott, director of Women and Gender Studies Institute at the University of Toronto, said this about the kneeling gesture:

“The kneel has been a kneel about articulating the promise and desire of freedom from oppression.”
– Rinaldo Walcott

So is “taking the knee” a valid form of protest or should the athletes who do it be fined and fired by their clubs?

In our opinion kneeling down to protest racism and oppression is a very honorable thing to do, especially when you could be fined for your actions or even lose your job in the NFL. Since racism and oppression can sometimes be taboo topics, it is even more important for people with a big platform, to create awareness for these topics, even if the way you have to do it is provocative and disliked by some groups of people. Thus the fact that they “took the knee” during the national anthem was a clever idea, because it created a large number of reactions and a whole debate broke out. Furthermore the fact that we are talking about the debate and the importance of its message, shows that the protests actually worked really well.

But there is still the question, whether or not most Americans really talk about the message behind the protests or just about the fact that someone dared to disrespect their flag on national TV. Or in other words, are the protests dividing the country in half because of the message or the execution?
We think that no matter what the real answer to this question is, the protests succeeded in making at least some people think about their message and this counts.

At the end of the day every person who was inspired by these protests is one person who might decide to fight for equality and against racial oppression and this idea can strengthen anyone who suffers from discrimination, institutionalized racism, etc. and give them hope that one day things will change.

But what do you think, is “taking the knee” an important and legitimate form of protest, or should sports and politics be separated and athletes who kneel down be fined for their actions?
